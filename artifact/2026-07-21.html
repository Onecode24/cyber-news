Beginner's Corner · not breaking news — a story to learn from

The ransomware attack that emptied gas stations up and down the US East Coast

The 2021 Colonial Pipeline attack, explained

This week's Coca-Cola/Fairlife story is a good moment to look back at the incident that first showed the public just how disruptive ransomware could be. In May 2021, a criminal group called DarkSide broke into Colonial Pipeline, the company operating the largest fuel pipeline in the United States, using a single compromised password for an old VPN account — one that didn't even have multi-factor authentication turned on.

The attackers encrypted Colonial's billing and business IT systems. Even though the pipeline's operational controls weren't directly hit, Colonial shut the pipeline down anyway because it couldn't safely bill customers for fuel — and that single decision cut off nearly half the East Coast's gasoline, diesel, and jet fuel supply. Panic buying emptied gas stations from Texas to Virginia for days.

Colonial paid the attackers roughly $4.4 million in Bitcoin to get a decryption tool, and the US Department of Justice later recovered about $2.3 million of that ransom by tracing the cryptocurrency wallet — one of the first times law enforcement clawed back ransomware payments at scale.

The lesson for beginners: this wasn't a sophisticated zero-day exploit, it was one leaked password on an account without MFA. It's why "basic hygiene" — multi-factor authentication, retiring unused accounts, and network segmentation between IT and operational systems — gets repeated so often in security training. Critical infrastructure attacks also don't need to touch the physical equipment to cause physical-world chaos; disrupting the business side is often enough.
