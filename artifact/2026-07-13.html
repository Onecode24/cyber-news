Beginner Pick

Learn from history — not breaking news

The Colonial Pipeline attack: when ransomware left the screen and hit the gas pump

In May 2021, a criminal ransomware gang called DarkSide broke into Colonial Pipeline, the company running the largest fuel pipeline in the eastern United States, using a single compromised password for an old VPN account that didn't have multi-factor authentication turned on. The attackers encrypted the company's billing systems, and out of caution Colonial shut down the entire pipeline for several days — not because the pipeline itself was hacked, but because they couldn't confirm the attack hadn't spread and couldn't bill customers if it kept running.

The shutdown triggered fuel shortages and panic-buying across the southeastern US, and Colonial ultimately paid roughly $4.4 million in ransom (some of which the FBI later recovered). It became one of the clearest real-world demonstrations that a cyberattack on a company's ordinary office network can cascade into empty gas stations hundreds of miles away.

Why it matters for newcomers: it's the go-to example of how "just" stolen credentials and missing MFA on one account can shut down critical infrastructure — and why basic hygiene (unique passwords, MFA everywhere, retiring unused accounts) is the unglamorous foundation of security.
